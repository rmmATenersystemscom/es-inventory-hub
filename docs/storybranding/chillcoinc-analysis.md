## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

ChillCo, Inc. is a Gulf South-based commercial and industrial refrigeration company founded in 1996, specializing in large-tonnage chiller systems and process cooling solutions. The company became 100% employee-owned in 2013 and serves commercial facilities, industrial plants, data centers, and specialized cooling applications across the Gulf South region.

**Key Findings:**
- Strong operational foundation (29 years, employee-owned)
- Clear service offerings (parts, rentals, field service, training)
- Good initial StoryBrand elements (customer testimonials, employee ownership story)
- Limited content depth for AI discoverability
- Significant opportunity to become Gulf South's chiller authority
- Regional expertise in hurricane-prone areas (unique angle)

#### 4. Glossary / Definitions

Create `/resources/chiller-glossary` with 100+ terms:

**Essential Chiller Terms:**

- **Chiller:** A refrigeration system that removes heat from a liquid (typically water or glycol) which is then circulated through air handlers or process equipment to provide cooling. Commercial chillers range from 20 to 2,000+ tons capacity.

- **Ton (of Cooling):** A unit of cooling capacity equal to 12,000 BTU/hour, or the amount of energy needed to melt one ton of ice in 24 hours. A 100-ton chiller removes 1,200,000 BTU/hour of heat.

- **kW/ton:** Energy efficiency measurement - kilowatts of electricity consumed per ton of cooling provided. Lower is better. Efficient water-cooled chillers: 0.45-0.60 kW/ton. Air-cooled: 0.85-1.20 kW/ton.

- **EER (Energy Efficiency Ratio):** Cooling capacity (BTU/hr) divided by power input (watts) at specific test conditions (95°F outdoor temp). Higher EER = more efficient. Formula: EER = 12 / (kW/ton).

- **IPLV (Integrated Part Load Value):** Weighted efficiency measurement that accounts for part-load operation (most chillers run at part load most of the time). IPLV is more representative of annual efficiency than full-load EER.

- **Approach Temperature:** Difference between leaving chilled water temperature and refrigerant evaporating temperature. Typical approach: 2-4°F. Increasing approach indicates fouled tubes or low refrigerant charge.

- **Delta T (ΔT):** Temperature difference between entering and leaving water. Chilled water: typically 10-12°F delta T. Condenser water: typically 10°F delta T. Decreasing delta T indicates flow problems.

- **Centrifugal Chiller:** High-efficiency chiller using centrifugal compressor technology. Best for large capacities (150-2,000+ tons). Most efficient type (0.45-0.55 kW/ton) but requires clean, oil-free refrigerant system.

- **Screw Chiller:** Chiller using rotary screw compressor. Good for mid-range capacities (75-800 tons). More tolerant of liquid refrigerant than centrifugal. Efficiency: 0.55-0.70 kW/ton.

- **Water-Cooled Chiller:** Chiller that rejects heat to water (requires cooling tower). More efficient than air-cooled but requires water treatment and additional equipment. Typical in large facilities.

- **Air-Cooled Chiller:** Chiller that rejects heat directly to outdoor air (no cooling tower needed). Less efficient but simpler installation. Common in smaller facilities or where water is scarce.

- **Cooling Tower:** Heat rejection equipment that cools condenser water through evaporative cooling. Water is sprayed over fill while air is drawn through, evaporating ~1% of water to cool the remaining 99%.

- **Refrigerant:** Working fluid in the refrigeration cycle. Common types: R-134a, R-410A, R-513A (HFO), R-1233zd. Some older systems use R-22 (being phased out) or ammonia (R-717) in industrial applications.

- **Compressor:** The "heart" of the chiller that compresses refrigerant vapor, raising its pressure and temperature. Types: centrifugal, screw, scroll, reciprocating. Compressor failure is the most costly chiller repair.

- **Evaporator:** Heat exchanger where refrigerant evaporates, absorbing heat from chilled water. Tube fouling reduces efficiency and capacity. Should be cleaned annually.

- **Condenser:** Heat exchanger where refrigerant condenses, rejecting heat to condenser water or outdoor air. Fouling or high entering temp reduces efficiency and capacity.
